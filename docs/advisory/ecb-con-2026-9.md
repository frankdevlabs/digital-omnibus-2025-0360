# ECB opinion — CON/2026/9 (Digital Omnibus)

A digest of the **European Central Bank** opinion on the *Digital Omnibus* proposal
(**COM(2025) 837 / 2025/0360 (COD)**). The ECB is a **mandatory consultee** under the proposal; this
opinion is an advisory input and not part of the Commission proposal.

> Source: committed opinion
> [`../../sources/advisory/ECB-CON-2026-9_opinion_2026-03-10.pdf`](../../sources/advisory/ECB-CON-2026-9_opinion_2026-03-10.pdf)
> (copy from the ECB website). Working digest — verify against the authoritative text on EUR-Lex
> (CELEX 52026AB0009). Paragraph numbers below are the opinion's own (1.x–4.x).

## Document facts

- **Reference:** Opinion CON/2026/9, **of 10 March 2026**, adopted by the Governing Council
  (Art 17.5 Rules of Procedure); signed by President **Christine Lagarde**, Frankfurt am Main.
- **Trigger:** request from the **European Parliament on 9 December 2025**.
- **Competence:** Articles **127(4) and 282(5) TFEU** — the proposal touches monetary policy
  (Art 127(2) first indent), the smooth operation of **payment systems** (Art 127(2) fourth indent),
  **prudential supervision** of credit institutions (Art 127(6)), and the ECB's **statistical**
  task (Art 5 Statute of the ESCB).
- **Transmission to Council:** cover note **ST 7244/26** (11 March 2026); see the register entries
  [`ECB-CON-2026-9`](../../sources/README.md) and [`ST-7244-2026`](../../sources/README.md).
- **Accompanied by** a separate **technical working document** with specific drafting proposals
  (available in English on EUR-Lex).

## Overall stance

The ECB **fully supports** the proposed regulation as *"an important reform"* and a *"first step"* to
simplify and optimise the digital rulebook, which has become *"complex … fragmented and overlapping"*.
It welcomes the consolidation of data-sharing rules in the Data Act, clearer definitions, and the
repeal of outdated instruments (notably Reg (EU) 2018/1807 on the free flow of non-personal data)
— see the [repeals & final-provisions provision](../provisions/final-repeals-p2b.md).
**However**, it has concerns *"in specific cases where it does not alleviate the burden of regulatory
compliance"* and offers technical observations — most pointedly on **DORA / incident reporting**.

## Key points on the data/cyber Omnibus (2025/0360)

### Data Act — business-to-government data sharing (§2.1–2.11)
- Welcomes simplification of G2B/B2G data sharing; NCBs (as public-sector bodies) and the ECB benefit
  from clearer availability of data and **high-value datasets** (used for statistics and the RIAD
  register). → [Data Act extract](../../extracts/commission/COM-2025-837_dataact-art1.md)
- On the new **Art 15a / Chapter VIIc** "exceptional need" power: the ECB **supports** narrowing it but
  asks to **simplify the conditions** public authorities must meet and to **clarify the definition of
  "public emergency"** (align it with — and make it no more restrictive than — Reg (EU) 2024/2747
  IMERA and Decision 2014/415/EU; a *threat* of emergency should suffice where the task is mitigation).
- **Compensation (Art 20):** welcomes free-of-charge access in emergencies, but data should be supplied
  **at cost, without a "reasonable margin"** for public-interest requests.

### Data protection — GDPR / EUDPR coherence (§3.1–3.12)
- The ECB processes under the **EUDPR** while NCBs/NCAs process under the **GDPR**, so one operation is
  governed by two instruments (e.g. TIPS, the future digital euro). It **welcomes the GDPR and EUDPR
  being amended in parallel** for coherence. → [EUDPR mirror provision](../provisions/eudpr-mirror.md)
- **Single entry point (SEP) for breach notification:** supports it and the *"report once, share many"*
  principle. → [single-entry-point provision](../provisions/cyber-single-entry-point.md) ·
  [Art 33 breach-notification provision](../provisions/gdpr-art33-breach-notification.md)
- Recommends **including the EUDPR** among the acts for which the SEP must be used (today the ECB still
  reports to the **EDPS** via the EUDPR channel).
- **Joint controllership:** a single breach could otherwise trigger **up to 22 near-identical
  notifications** (ECB + all Eurosystem NCBs) — contrary to simplification. Proposes that joint
  controllers be **free to file one notification via the SEP on behalf of all**, and that no
  notification be required where the breach is unlikely to risk rights and freedoms.

### Incident reporting / DORA — the ECB's main reservation (§4.1–4.6)
- Supports harmonised, centralised ICT-incident reporting **in principle**, but warns the SEP **does
  not actually cut the burden**: different taxonomies, templates and procedures still apply under each
  framework, so a financial entity must still prepare separate reports (e.g. GDPR vs DORA) — merely
  sending them to one recipient does not help. → [cyber extract](../../extracts/commission/COM-2025-837_cyber-art6-9.md)
- DORA reports trigger **time-critical crisis processes**; routing them through a new actor/system risks
  delay — they should go **directly to the competent authority**.
- Significant resources were already invested in DORA (applied **17 January 2025**).
- **Recommendation:** **exclude DORA from the proposed regulation** for now — gain experience
  separately with the new SEP (covering the other instruments, including the EUDPR) and the DORA
  regime, then integrate later. Concrete drafting amendments are provided in the technical annex.

## Why it matters here

The ECB opinion is narrower than the EDPB–EDPS or EESC opinions — it speaks only to the ECB's own
fields of competence — but it lands directly on two strands this repo tracks: the **single entry
point / incident reporting** and **personal-data-breach notification**. Its headline ask (carve **DORA**
out of the single-entry-point regime, at least initially) and its joint-controllership "22
notifications" point are concrete signals to watch in the Council's cyber discussions.
