# Extract — ST 10729/26, Article 3 (amendments to the GDPR, Reg. (EU) 2016/679)

> **Source:** Council document **ST 10729/26** (LIMITE), Brussels, **22 June 2026** — Presidency
> *Mandate for negotiations with the European Parliament* (General Secretariat → Permanent Representatives
> Committee), file 2025/0360 (COD). **This is a working transcription, not an official text.** It reproduces
> the *consolidated* reading (the Presidency's tracked changes accepted). In the source, **bold/strikethrough**
> marks changes vs the Commission proposal and **underline** marks changes vs the previous compromise
> **ST 10677/26 (18 June 2026)**. Salient changes vs the prior tracked version **ST 9547/26** are flagged
> with **▸**; whole-provision deletions are marked **[DELETED in ST 10729/26]**; undecided source brackets
> `[...]` are preserved. Always verify against the authoritative document. See [`../../NOTICE`](../../NOTICE).

*Regulation (EU) 2016/679 is amended as follows.* This is the **fifth** Presidency compromise; per the
Presidency NOTE the text amends **fourteen GDPR articles**. The headline new item vs ST 9547/26 is a
**new Article 49 derogation amendment** (point 10a) plus interpretive **recital 40b** for third-country
tax-data transfers — see [recitals](ST-10729-2026_recitals.md#recital-40b--third-country-tax-transfers)
and [provision page](../../docs/provisions/gdpr-art49-third-country-tax-transfers.md).

---

<a id="point-1--article-4-definitions"></a>
## Point 1 — Article 4 (definitions)

**(a) Addition to Art 4(1) on the concept of personal data — [DELETED in ST 10729/26].**
The Commission's proposed sentences ("Information relating to a natural person is not necessarily
personal data for every other person or entity… Information shall not be personal for a given entity
where that entity cannot identify the natural person…") remain **struck out**. Article 4(1) therefore
**remains unchanged** from the current GDPR. See [provision page](../../docs/provisions/gdpr-art4-personal-data.md).

**(b) New definitions added to Article 4 — only (38) survives:**

- **(38) 'scientific research'** = any research conducted in an autonomous and independent manner, with the
  aim of contributing to the growth of society's general knowledge and wellbeing, generating new or
  complementing existing scientific knowledge, following a methodological and systematic approach consistent
  with standards of the relevant scientific field, including ethical standards, and producing verifiable and
  transparent results.

▸ **Change vs ST 9547/26:** the cookie-support definitions **(32) 'terminal equipment', (33) 'electronic
communications networks', (34) 'web browser', (35) 'media service', (36) 'media service provider',
(37) 'online interface' are now all [DELETED in ST 10729/26]** (struck). They are no longer needed because
the cookie/terminal-equipment regime has left the GDPR entirely (Articles 88a–88c all deleted — see Point 15).
Only the **(38) scientific-research** definition is added.

---

## Point 2 — Article 5(1)(b) (purpose limitation)

Replaced by: *"collected for specified, explicit and legitimate purposes and not further processed in a
manner that is incompatible with those purposes; further processing for archiving purposes in the public
interest, scientific or historical research purposes or statistical purposes shall, subject to the
application of appropriate safeguards in accordance with Article 89(1), be considered to be compatible
with the initial purposes, independent of the conditions of Article 6(4) of this Regulation
('purpose limitation');"* — unchanged vs ST 9547/26.

---

<a id="point-3--article-9-special-categories"></a>
## Point 3 — Article 9 (special categories of personal data)

**(a) New points added to Article 9(2):**

- **(k)** incidental and residual processing in the context of the **development and technical operation**
  of an AI system as defined in Article 3(1) of Regulation (EU) 2024/1689, or an AI model as referred to in
  that Regulation, subject to the conditions in paragraph 5.
- **(l)** processing of biometric data **strictly necessary** for confirming the identity of a data subject
  (**verification**), where the biometric data or the means needed for the **one-to-one** verification is
  under the **sole control of the data subject** and, where applicable, subject to appropriate safeguards as
  laid down in Union or Member State law, in accordance with paragraph 4.

**(b) New Article 9(5):**
*"For processing referred to in point (k) of paragraph 2, appropriate organisational and technical measures
shall be implemented to avoid the collection and otherwise processing of special categories of personal
data. Where, despite the implementation of such measures, the controller identifies special categories of
personal data that are incidentally and residually involved in the datasets used for training, testing or
validation or in the AI system or AI model, the controller shall **erase** such data without undue delay. If
erasure of those data proves to be technically impossible or requires manifestly disproportionate effort,
the controller shall, without undue delay and in any event, effectively protect such data from being further
processed or processed for other purposes, used to produce outputs, disclosed or otherwise made available to
third parties."*
▸ **Change vs ST 9547/26:** the explicit **regular-verification + life-cycle-documentation** sentence that
closed ST 9547/26's Article 9(5) is **dropped**; "(k)" now reads "development and **technical** operation".

---

<a id="point-4--article-12-5-abusive-requests"></a>
## Point 4 — Article 12(5) (manifestly unfounded / excessive / abusive requests)

Replaced by: *"Information provided under Articles 13 and 14 and any communication and any actions taken
under Articles 15 to 22 and 34 shall be provided free of charge. Where requests from a data subject are
manifestly unfounded or excessive, in particular because of their repetitive character or where an
**abusive intention** on the part of the data subject submitting those requests can be demonstrated, the
controller may either: (a) charge a reasonable fee proportionate to the administrative costs…; or (b) refuse
to act on the request and inform the data subject of the reasons thereof. The controller shall bear the
burden of demonstrating, in the light of all the relevant circumstances of the case, that the request is
manifestly unfounded or excessive."*
▸ **Change vs ST 9547/26:** the abusive-intention test is **no longer limited to "requests under Article
15"** — it now applies generally. The controller keeps the burden of proof.

---

<a id="point-5--article-13-5-information-obligations"></a>
## Point 5 — Article 13(5) (information-obligation exemption, new paragraph)

Added as **Article 13(5)**, restructured into cumulative conditions (a)–(d): paragraphs 1 and 2 do not apply
where **(a)** there are reasonable grounds to assume the data subject already has the information under
points (a) and (c) of paragraph 1 **and** the data are collected in the context of a **direct and clearly
circumscribed relationship** between data subjects and a controller; **(b)** the processing is not likely to
result in a **high risk** within the meaning of Article 35; **(c)** the processing does not involve complex
operations, large amounts of personal data, special categories, or criminal-conviction data; and **(d)** the
controller does not transmit the data to other recipients, transfer it to a third country, or carry out
Article 22(1) automated decision-making.
▸ **Change vs ST 9547/26:** renumbered **13(4) → 13(5)** and recast from a prose exemption into the explicit
four-condition (a)–(d) test above.

## Point 6 — Article 13(6) (research exemption, new paragraph)

Added: *"When the further processing takes place for scientific research purposes by the same controller and
where and insofar as the provision of information referred to under paragraphs 1, 2 and 3 proves impossible
or would involve a disproportionate effort, or insofar as the obligation… is likely to render impossible or
seriously impair the achievement of the objectives of that further processing, subject to the conditions and
safeguards referred to in Article 89(1), the controller does not need to provide the information… In such
cases the controller shall take appropriate measures to protect the data subject's rights and freedoms and
legitimate interests, including making the information publicly available."* — substance as ST 9547/26
(renumbered to 13(6)).

---

<a id="point-7--article-22-automated-decisions"></a>
## Point 7 — Article 22 (automated individual decision-making)

Replaced. Consolidated: *"1. The data subject shall have the right **not to be subject to** a decision based
solely on automated processing, including profiling, which produces legal effects concerning him or her or
similarly significantly affects him or her, unless such processing: (a) is necessary for entering into, or
performance of, a contract between the data subject and a data controller; (b) is authorised by Union or
Member State law… ; or (c) is based on the data subject's explicit consent. 2. In the cases referred to in
points (a) and (c) of paragraph 1, the data controller shall implement suitable measures to safeguard the
data subject's rights and freedoms and legitimate interests, at least the right to obtain human intervention…
3. Decisions referred to in paragraph 1 shall not be based on special categories of personal data referred
to in Article 9(1), unless point (a) or (g) of Article 9(2) applies and suitable measures… are in place."*
▸ Article 22 remains **largely restored to the current GDPR** (the Commission's "regardless of whether the
decision could be taken otherwise than by solely automated means" clause stays struck). Unchanged vs ST 9547/26.

## Point 7a — Article 25 (data protection by design and by default)

Paragraphs 1 and 2 replaced so the obligations bind **"the controller and the processor"**, with explicit
reference to the lists under Article 35(4)/(5). Substance otherwise as the current Article 25. Unchanged vs
ST 9547/26.

---

<a id="point-8--article-33-breach-notification"></a>
## Point 8 — Article 33 (personal-data-breach notification)

**(a) Article 33(1) replaced:** *"In the case of a personal data breach that is **likely to result in a high
risk**… the controller shall without undue delay and, where feasible, not later than **96 hours** after
having become aware of it, notify the personal data breach **via the national entry point established
pursuant to Article 23b of Directive (EU) 2022/2555** to the supervisory authority competent in accordance
with Articles 55 and 56. Where the notification… is not made within 96 hours, it shall be accompanied by
reasons for the delay."*

**(b) New Article 33(1a):** until the national entry point is established under Article 23b of Directive (EU)
2022/2555, controllers continue to notify the competent supervisory authority **directly**.

**(c) New Article 33(6), (7) and (7a):** the **Board** shall establish and make public a common breach-
notification template and the two high-risk / not-high-risk lists, available within `[OP date = nine months
of the entry into application of this Regulation]`; reviewed at least every three years; the Commission may
adopt the template by implementing act (Art 93(2)).
▸ 96-hour / high-risk-only threshold and the national-entry-point routing are **carried over** from ST 9547/26
(both were already Commission/Council positions). The entry-point article itself (NIS2 Art 23b) was tweaked
for flexibility this round — see [cyber extract](ST-10729-2026_cyber-art6-9.md) and
[recital 49](ST-10729-2026_recitals.md#recital-49--national-entry-point).

---

<a id="point-9--article-35-dpia-lists"></a>
## Point 9 — Article 35 (data protection impact assessment)

**(a) Paragraphs 4, 5, 6 replaced:** the **Board** shall establish and make public (i) the list of processing
operations subject to a DPIA, (ii) the list of those for which no DPIA is required, and (iii) a common
template and methodology.
**(b) New paragraphs 6a–6c:** lists/template/methodology to be published within `[OP date = 9 months of the
entry into application of this Regulation]`; the Commission may adopt the template by implementing act; review
at least every three years; existing national DPA lists remain valid until the Board publishes the EU lists.
▸ Carried over from ST 9547/26 (power moved Commission → EDPB; single EU-level lists). Unchanged in substance.

## Point 9a — Article 37 (DPO contact details) — [DELETED in ST 10729/26]

▸ **Change vs ST 9547/26.** The proposed amendment to **Article 37** — the obligation to communicate the
contact details of the Data Protection Officer to the supervisory authority — is **deleted** this round, so
Article 37 is **maintained as currently laid down** in the GDPR (Presidency NOTE ¶11(i)).

---

<a id="point-10--article-29a-pseudonymisation"></a>
## Point 10 — New Article 29a (application of pseudonymisation and identification of a natural person)

*"(1) Controllers and processors may apply pseudonymisation in order to reduce the risks to the data subjects
concerned and to comply with their obligations under this Regulation, in such a manner that the personal data
can no longer be attributed to a specific data subject without the use of additional information. To determine
whether a natural person is identifiable, including through personal data having undergone pseudonymisation,
account shall be taken of all the means reasonably likely to be used, such as **singling out** or online
identifiers, either by the controller or by another person, to identify the natural person directly or
indirectly.
(2) The application of pseudonymisation to personal data may, depending on the circumstances of the case and
provided that appropriate technical and organisational measures are put in place… effectively prevent persons
other than the controller from identifying the data subject in such a way that, for them, the data subject is
not or is no longer identifiable.
**(2a) Where a person other than the controller referred to in paragraph 2 discloses, transmits or otherwise
makes such data available to a third party and it cannot be ruled out that this third party possesses or can
obtain means reasonably likely to enable the data subject to be identified, both the transmission of the data
to this third party and the subsequent processing of the data by this third party is to be considered as
processing of data relating to an identifiable natural person.**
(3) Paragraphs 1 and 2 are without prejudice to other provisions and obligations applicable to the controller,
including under Chapters IV and V… The provision set out in paragraph 2 shall not apply to processors.
(4) The Board shall issue an opinion, in accordance with Article 64(2), addressing the application of
pseudonymisation and anonymisation…
(5) The Chair of the Board shall request the opinion referred to in paragraph 4 no later than **6 months**
after the entry into force of this Regulation. The opinion shall be reviewed and updated where necessary."*
▸ **Changes vs ST 9547/26:** **new paragraph (2a)** (the underlined edit vs ST 10677/26) — pseudonymised data
passed on by a non-controller to a third party who can re-identify is treated as personal data for both the
transmission and the third party's subsequent processing; the opinion-request deadline tightens from **12 → 6
months**. Supporting recital **27a** is updated accordingly — see
[recitals](ST-10729-2026_recitals.md#recital-27a--27b-pseudonymisation).

---

<a id="point-10a--article-49-tax-transfers"></a>
## Point 10a — Article 49 (derogations for specific situations) — **NEW in ST 10729/26**

**(a) Article 49(1)(d) replaced:** *"(d) the transfer is necessary for important reasons of public interest
**as recognised in Union law or in the law of the Member State to which the controller is subject**;"*
**(b) Article 49(4) deleted.**

▸ **This provision is entirely new vs ST 9547/26 and is the headline change in this text.** GDPR Chapter V
(international transfers) was **untouched by the Commission proposal**; the Presidency added this at "several
delegations'" request via the Antici Group (NOTE ¶12). Read with interpretive **recital 40b**, it brings
**periodic/automated transfers of personal data to third countries for tax cooperation** (FATCA-style — e.g.
bank data to the US IRS) under the Article 49(1)(d) public-interest derogation. No impact assessment was
conducted. See [recital 40b](ST-10729-2026_recitals.md#recital-40b--third-country-tax-transfers) and the
[provision page](../../docs/provisions/gdpr-art49-third-country-tax-transfers.md). Live context: pending CJEU
case **C-804/25** (FATCA v GDPR).

---

<a id="point-11--article-57-supervisory-authority-requests"></a>
## Point 11 — Article 57 (tasks of the supervisory authority)

- **(a)** Article 57(1) point (k) **deleted**.
- **(ab)** Article 57(4) replaced: *"Where requests are manifestly unfounded or excessive, in particular
  because of their repetitive character or where an **abusive intention** on the part of the data subject
  submitting those requests can be demonstrated, the supervisory authority may charge a reasonable fee based
  on administrative costs, or refuse to act on the request. The supervisory authority shall, in the light of
  all the relevant circumstances of the case, bear the burden of demonstrating the manifestly unfounded or
  excessive character of the request."* — unchanged vs ST 9547/26.

## Points 12–14 — Articles 64 & 70 (EDPB tasks and consistency)

- **12.** Article 64(1) point (a) deleted.
- **12a.** Article 70(1)(f) amended — guidelines on the criteria for **profiling** decisions under Art 22(1).
- **13.** Article 70(1)(h) deleted.
- **14.** New points inserted in Article 70(1): **(ha)** establish the DPIA lists; **(hb)** establish the
  DPIA template/methodology; **(hc)** establish the breach-notification template and high-risk lists;
  **(hca)** issue guidelines on Article 32 security measures; **(hcb)** issue the pseudonymisation/
  anonymisation opinion under Article 29a. Unchanged vs ST 9547/26.

---

<a id="point-15--articles-88-cookies-ai-li"></a>
## Point 15 — Articles 88a / 88b / 88c — **all [DELETED in ST 10729/26]**

The Commission proposed three new articles after Article 88. In ST 10729/26 the **entire block ("After
Article 88, the following articles are added") is struck out**:

- **Article 88a (terminal-equipment / cookie consent) — [DELETED].** The cookie-consent regime stays in the
  **ePrivacy Directive** (amended Art 5(3)) — see [`ST-10729-2026_eprivacy-art5.md`](ST-10729-2026_eprivacy-art5.md)
  and [cookies provision](../../docs/provisions/gdpr-art88a-88b-cookies.md).
- **Article 88b (machine-readable / automated consent signal) — [DELETED in ST 10729/26].**
  ▸ **Change vs ST 9547/26**, where Article 88b was **retained**. The standalone automated/centralised
  consent-signal duty (browser/OS technical means, staged application) is **dropped** this round — consistent
  with the concern Coreper raised on 8 June about "the proposed new provision mandating an automated and
  centralised consent signal for the placing of cookies and the absence of impact assessment" (NOTE ¶8). The
  consequential cross-reference to Art 88b in the amended ePrivacy Article 5 is struck accordingly.
- **Article 88c (AI legitimate interest) — [DELETED].** Survives only as interpretive **recital 33a**; recitals
  30 and 31 remain deleted — see
  [`ST-10729-2026_recitals.md`](ST-10729-2026_recitals.md#recital-33a-ai-legitimate-interest) and
  [AI LI provision](../../docs/provisions/gdpr-art88c-ai-legitimate-interest.md).
