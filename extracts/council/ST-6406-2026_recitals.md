# Extract — ST 6406/26, key recitals (curated subset)

> **Source:** Council document **ST 6406/26** (LIMITE), Brussels, 20 February 2026 — Presidency
> compromise text, for the AGS meeting (Antici Group (Simplification)) of 27 February 2026.
> Interinstitutional file 2025/0360 (COD). **This is a working transcription, not an official text** —
> consolidated reading (Presidency tracked changes against the Commission proposal accepted: additions
> in bold kept, ~~strikethrough~~ deletions dropped). **This is a curated subset** of the recitals (those
> carrying the substantive choices tracked in this repo), summarised in their consolidated sense, not the
> full preamble. Recital numbers follow **this document's own** numbering, which differs from the May
> text. Always verify against the authoritative document. See [`../../NOTICE`](../../NOTICE).

Recitals are interpretive, not operative. The February preamble (as renumbered in this text) runs from
recital **(27)** to **(61)**, with a gap from **(48)** to **(58)** (the intervening recitals concern
instrument amendments not carried in this compromise). Salient changes are flagged with **▸**. In this
first compromise the Presidency **deleted** several Commission recitals and **added** new ones; those are
marked **[DELETED in ST 6406/26]** / **[NEW in ST 6406/26]**.

---

## Recital 27 — concept of personal data — [DELETED in ST 6406/26]
The Commission's long recital reframing personal data toward a **relative / controller-centric** concept
(that information "is not to be considered personal data for a given entity" lacking the means to identify;
that a later transmission to a third party with such means renders it personal only for that third party;
the implementing-act support) is **struck out in its entirety**.
▸ Consistent with the operative **Article 4(1) addition also being deleted**
([GDPR point 1](ST-6406-2026_gdpr-art3-amendments.md#point-1--article-4-definitions)). The identifiability
guidance the Commission attached here is not lost entirely — a reworked version is added as the new
recital 27a (below). See [personal-data provision page](../../docs/provisions/gdpr-art4-personal-data.md).

<a id="recital-27a-pseudonymisation"></a>
## Recital 27a — identifiability / pseudonymisation / EDPB guidelines — [NEW in ST 6406/26]
**Wholly new recital.** *Consolidated sense:* to determine whether a natural person is identifiable,
account should be taken of all the **means reasonably likely to be used** to identify them, directly or
indirectly; the **European Data Protection Board** should support controllers by adopting **guidelines**
on the state of the art of techniques and the technical/organisational measures and criteria to
pseudonymise personal data effectively, clarifying when a person is identifiable and whether data
resulting from pseudonymisation may no longer constitute personal data for certain entities; controllers
remain responsible for determining whether such data are personal.
▸ Anchors the new **Article 70(1)(hca)** EDPB-guidelines task (point 14). Note this version has **no
operative pseudonymisation article** — the Commission's Article 41a is deleted
([GDPR point 10](ST-6406-2026_gdpr-art3-amendments.md#point-10--article-41a-pseudonymisation)); by May an
EDPB-centred **Article 29a** is added instead.

## Recital 28 — scientific-research conditions — [DELETED in ST 6406/26]
The Commission's recital on assessing whether research meets the conditions of scientific research
(methodological/systematic approach; conduct in academic, industry and other settings incl. SMEs;
Article 179(2) TFEU) is **struck out**.

## Recital 29 — further processing (archiving / research / statistics); Article 89 — retained, amended
*Consolidated sense:* further processing for archiving in the public interest, scientific or historical
research or statistical purposes should be considered compatible; it **should not be necessary** to
ascertain on the basis of Article 6(4) of Regulation (EU) 2016/679 whether the further-processing purpose
is compatible. **Such further processing should be considered compatible, provided** it complies with the
**purpose-limitation** principle and the **Article 89** safeguards; the qualification as scientific
research should be based on **objective characteristics** of the research activity, not solely the
controller's declaration.
▸ The Presidency rewrote the tail (added the "should be considered compatible, provided … Article 89"
block); the deleted scientific-research *definition* (recital 28) is partly recast into these objective
criteria.

<a id="recital-30-31-ai-legitimate-interest"></a>
## Recital 30 — AI and legitimate interest (RETAINED in February)
**Present (unchanged Commission text).** *Consolidated sense:* trustworthy AI supports economic growth and
socially beneficial innovation; the development and use of AI systems and underlying models (e.g. large
language models, generative video models) rely on data, including personal data, across the AI life cycle
(training, testing, validation, and retention in the system/model). Such processing **may therefore be
carried out for a legitimate interest** within the meaning of **Article 6** of Regulation (EU) 2016/679,
where appropriate — without affecting the controller's duty to comply with all other law and the other
conditions of Article 6(1)(f).

## Recital 31 — balancing test for AI legitimate interest (RETAINED in February)
**Present (unchanged Commission text).** *Consolidated sense:* when balancing the controller's/third
party's legitimate interest against the data subject's interests, rights and freedoms, consideration
should be given to whether the interest is beneficial for the data subject and society (e.g. detecting and
removing bias; accurate, safe outputs; improved accessibility), to the **reasonable expectations** of the
data subject, and to **appropriate safeguards** — enhanced transparency, an **unconditional right to
object**, respecting technical indications limiting third-party use of the data, privacy-preserving
techniques for AI training, and measures to minimise risks from **regurgitation, data leakage** and other
foreseeable actions.

▸ **Major Feb-vs-May divergence.** Recitals 30 and 31 are **both present** in February, supporting the
standalone operative AI legitimate-interest article (**Article 88c**, also present here — see
[GDPR point 15](ST-6406-2026_gdpr-art3-amendments.md#point-15--articles-88-machine-readable-consent)). By
May, **recitals 30 and 31 are deleted** and Article 88c is deleted; the statement survives there only as
**recital 33a**. See [May recitals](ST-9547-2026_recitals.md#recital-33a-ai-legitimate-interest) and the
[AI LI provision page](../../docs/provisions/gdpr-art88c-ai-legitimate-interest.md).

## Recital 32 — scientific research as legitimate interest; Article 89(1) — retained, amended
*Consolidated sense:* processing for scientific-research purposes and the GDPR's research provisions are
conditional on **Article 89(1)** safeguards, balancing data protection (Art 8 CFREU) against the freedom
of science (Art 13 CFREU); such processing **may be necessary for the interests pursued by a controller or
a third party** within the meaning of **Article 6(1)(f)**, provided the research is not contrary to Union
or Member State law and may also follow the public interest.
▸ The Presidency reworked the legitimate-interest framing (from "pursues a legitimate interest" to "may be
necessary for the … interests pursued by a controller or by a third-party").

## Recital 33 — special categories of personal data in AI; Article 9(2) derogation (RETAINED)
**Present.** *Consolidated sense:* developing AI may involve special categories of personal data residually
present in training/testing/validation datasets; a **derogation from the Article 9(1) prohibition**
(operative Article 9(2)(k)) should be allowed where the controller implements effective technical and
organisational measures to avoid such processing and, once identified, **effectively removes** the data —
or, where removal needs disproportionate effort (e.g. re-engineering the model), protects it from being
used to infer outputs, disclosed or made available to third parties. The derogation does not apply where
processing special categories is itself necessary for the purpose (then Article 9(2)(a)–(j) apply).
See [special-categories provision page](../../docs/provisions/gdpr-art9-special-categories-ai.md).
▸ Uses **"remove"** (May's operative text changes this to **"erase"**).

## Recital 34 — biometric verification; Article 4(14) — retained, amended
*Consolidated sense:* biometric data allows or confirms unique identification; the **verification**
(one-to-one) function is distinguished from identification (one-to-many). Derogating from the Article 9(1)
prohibition is allowed where verification of the claimed identity is **necessary and proportionate** and
provided for under Union or Member State law, the less-intrusive means being chosen, and where the
biometric data or means are under the **sole control and possession** of the data subject (e.g. stored on
the data subject's device, or encrypted by the controller with the key held by the data subject).
Supports operative Article 9(2)(l).

## Recital 35 — manifestly unfounded / excessive / abusive access requests; Article 15 — retained, amended
*Consolidated sense:* the Article 15 right of access lets data subjects obtain confirmation and access;
Article 12(5) already provides that where a request is manifestly unfounded or excessive the controller
may charge a reasonable fee or refuse, and this should apply **also where an abusive intention** on the
part of the data subject can be **demonstrated by the controller** — e.g. requests aimed only at causing
damage/harm, or submitting numbers of identical or largely similar requests with the sole intent of
harming the controller.
See [abusive-requests provision page](../../docs/provisions/gdpr-art12-57-abusive-requests.md).
▸ The Presidency rewrote this around the **"abusive intention … demonstrated by the controller"** standard
(deleting the Commission's longer "abuse … lower burden of proof" passages). Unlike May, February carries
**no parallel Article 57(4)** amendment for supervisory authorities
([GDPR point 11](ST-6406-2026_gdpr-art3-amendments.md#point-11--article-57-supervisory-authority-requests)).

## Recital 36 — information-obligation exemption; Article 13(4) — retained, amended
*Consolidated sense:* the Article 13(4) derogation (no duty to inform where the data subject already has
the information) is extended to situations where processing is **not likely to result in a high risk**
(Article 35) and there are reasonable grounds to assume the data subject has the points (a)/(c) information,
in a **direct, limited and clearly circumscribed** relationship that **does not involve processing a large
amount of personal data** — e.g. a craftsman and their clients, associations and sports clubs managing
membership. The derogation must not undermine transparency and is without prejudice to Article 15 access.

## Recital 37 — research exemption from the information duty; Article 13(5) — retained, amended
*Consolidated sense:* where **further processing by the same controller** for scientific research makes
providing the Article 13 information impossible or disproportionate, it should not be necessary to provide
it; the controller should make reasonable efforts to obtain contact details and otherwise inform data
subjects indirectly (e.g. by making the information publicly available).

## Recital 38 — Article 22 (automated individual decision-making) — [DELETED in ST 6406/26]
The Commission's recital justifying the rewrite of **Article 22** (decisions based solely on automated
processing; the "the decision could also be taken by a human … use the less intrusive solution" rationale)
is **struck out in its entirety**.
▸ Consistent with the operative **Article 22 amendment also being deleted** in February (the article
remains unchanged from the current GDPR — see
[GDPR point 7](ST-6406-2026_gdpr-art3-amendments.md#point-7--article-22-automated-decisions)). Deleted in
May as well.

## Recital 39 — breach-notification threshold; EDPB template/lists; Article 33 — retained, amended
*Consolidated sense:* to reduce burden while keeping authorities informed, the threshold for notifying a
personal data breach to the supervisory authority under Article 33 should be **aligned to that of
communication to the data subject (high risk)** under Article 34; the higher threshold does not affect the
controller's duty to **document** the breach (Article 33(5)) or to demonstrate compliance (Article 5(2));
and the **Board** should **establish and make public** a common breach-notification template and the
high-risk / not-high-risk lists, reviewed at least every three years.
See [breach-notification provision page](../../docs/provisions/gdpr-art33-breach-notification.md).
▸ This recital is about the **threshold and the EDPB template/lists** only — it does **not** mention the
single-entry point. The single-entry-point routing appears solely (and in square brackets) in operative
**Article 33(1)/(1a)**; the entry point itself is not established by any article in February (no cyber
Articles 6–9 — see [cyber extract](ST-6406-2026_cyber-art6-9.md)).

## Recital 40 — DPIA lists; Article 35 — retained, amended
*Consolidated sense:* a **single EU-level list** of processing operations requiring a DPIA (and, made
mandatory, of those not requiring one) should replace national lists; these lists, plus a common template
and methodology, should be **established and made public by the Board** and reviewed at least every three
years.
▸ The Presidency removed the Commission's "Board prepares → Commission adopts by implementing act" wording
in favour of the Board establishing and publishing directly.

## Recitals 41–43 — Regulation (EU) 2018/1725 and Directive (EU) 2016/680 alignment (RETAINED)
**Present.** (41) the amendments to the GDPR should be brought into **Regulation (EU) 2018/1725** (EU
institutions) and aligned with **Directive (EU) 2016/680** (law-enforcement); (42) Regulation 2018/1725 is
amended in parallel, to be interpreted homogeneously with the GDPR; (43) the necessary adaptations of
Directive 2016/680 and other acts should follow so they apply as close as possible to these amendments.

---

## Recital 44 — terminal equipment / cookies — kept in the GDPR in February (RETAINED)
**Present.** *Consolidated sense:* the storing of, or access to, personal data in a natural person's
**terminal equipment** and the subsequent processing should be regulated under a **single legal framework,
namely Regulation (EU) 2016/679** (the new **GDPR Article 88a**) where the subscriber/user is a natural
person — covering cookies and similar technologies, regardless of who owns the equipment. Storage/access
is allowed on the basis of **consent**; a **limitative list** of low-risk / requested-service purposes is
permitted without consent; for other subsequent processing, Article 6 (and, where relevant, Article 9)
apply, including reliance on **legitimate interest** under Article 6(1)(f) subject to safeguards (taking
account of whether the data subject is a child, reasonable expectations, scale/sensitivity, no continuous
monitoring of private life), failing which **consent** under Articles 6–7 is needed.
See [cookies provision page](../../docs/provisions/gdpr-art88a-88b-cookies.md).
▸ **Feb-vs-May divergence.** February locates the cookie-consent regime **in the GDPR (Article 88a)**; May
moves it **back into the ePrivacy Directive (Article 5(3))** and deletes Article 88a. See
[ePrivacy extract](ST-6406-2026_eprivacy-art5.md).

## Recital 45 — consent fatigue / no-repeat after refusal (RETAINED)
**Present.** *Consolidated sense:* data subjects who refused consent are often re-prompted on each visit,
which may push them to consent just to stop the requests; the controller should therefore be obliged to
**respect a refusal for at least a certain period**.

## Recital 46 — automated and machine-readable indications of choice; web browsers (RETAINED)
**Present.** *Consolidated sense:* data subjects should be able to rely on **automated, machine-readable**
indications to consent, refuse or object; these may be implemented in **web browser** settings or in the
**EU Digital Identity Wallet** (Regulation (EU) 910/2014 — the source writes "914/2014"), or other adequate
means; controllers should respect such signals once standards exist; **media service providers** should not
be obliged to respect them (independent journalism).
▸ Supports operative **Article 88b** (machine-readable signals), which is **retained into May** (renumbered,
and there extended to operating-system providers).

---

## Recital 47 — ePrivacy Directive 2002/58/EC; Article 5(3) (RETAINED)
**Present.** *Consolidated sense:* the ePrivacy Directive's protections (incl. confidentiality of
communications) are preserved; the existing **Article 5(3) of Directive 2002/58/EC should remain
applicable** insofar as the subscriber or user is **not a natural person** and no personal data are
processed (for natural persons, the new GDPR Article 88a applies).

## Recital 48 — repeal of ePrivacy Article 4 (RETAINED)
**Present.** *Consolidated sense:* **Article 4 of Directive 2002/58/EC** (security of services; breach
notification by providers of electronic communications services) **should be repealed** — NIS2 (Directive
(EU) 2022/2555) now sets the cybersecurity/incident-reporting requirements, and GDPR Articles 32–34 cover
security of processing and breach notification, avoiding overlap.
▸ Stated in the recital only; **no operative amending article** carries it out in this text (see
[ePrivacy extract](ST-6406-2026_eprivacy-art5.md)).

---

## Recital 58 — EDPS / EDPB consultation — retained, amended
*Consolidated sense:* the **European Data Protection Supervisor and the European Data Protection Board**
were consulted in accordance with Article 42 of Regulation (EU) 2018/1725 and delivered their **joint
opinion on 10 February 2026**.
▸ The Presidency merged the separate EDPS/EDPB consultation recitals into a single joint-opinion recital
and filled in the date.

## Recital 59 — repeal of the Platform-to-Business Regulation (EU) 2019/1150 — retained, amended
*Consolidated sense:* the DSA (Regulation (EU) 2022/2065) and DMA (Regulation (EU) 2022/1925) provide a
harmonised framework largely covering the **P2B Regulation**, which **should be repealed**; for legal
certainty and to keep the necessary protection for **business users**, selected P2B provisions —
definitions in Article 2, the restriction/suspension rules in Article 4, the internal complaint-handling
system in Article 11 (that are cross-referenced by, or not covered by, other acts, in particular Directive
(EU) 2023/2831 on platform-work conditions), and Article 15 on enforcement — will **temporarily remain in
application until 2032**.
See [final provisions](ST-6406-2026_final-art10-11.md#article-10-amendments-repeals-transitory) and the
[repealed-instruments page](../../docs/instruments/repealed.md).

## Recital 61 — legal basis — [NEW in ST 6406/26]
**Wholly new recital.** The amendments to Regulation (EU) 2016/679 and Regulation (EU) 2018/1725 are based
on **Article 16 TFEU**; the amendments to Directive 2002/58/EC are based on **Article 16 TFEU and Article
114 TFEU**; all other amendments are based on **Article 114 TFEU**.
