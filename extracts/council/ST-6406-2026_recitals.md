# Extract — ST 6406/26, key recitals (curated subset)

> **Source:** Council document **ST 6406/26** (LIMITE), Brussels, 20 February 2026 — Presidency
> compromise text, for the AGS meeting (Antici Group (Simplification)) of 27 February 2026.
> Interinstitutional file 2025/0360 (COD). **This is a working transcription, not an official text** —
> consolidated reading (Presidency tracked changes against the Commission proposal accepted).
> **This is a curated subset** of the recitals (those carrying the substantive choices tracked in this
> repo), not the full preamble. Recital numbers follow **this document's own** numbering, which differs
> from the May text. Always verify against the authoritative document. See [`../../NOTICE`](../../NOTICE).

Recitals are interpretive, not operative. They are summarised here in their consolidated sense. The
February preamble runs from recital **(27)** to **(61)** (with a gap from (48) to (58) — the
intervening recitals belong to instrument amendments not carried in this compromise). Salient changes
are flagged with **▸**; deleted recitals are marked **[DELETED in ST 6406/26]**.

---

## Recital 27 (concept of personal data)
Retained. Restates that personal data is any information relating to an identified or identifiable
natural person, and that identifiability turns on **the means reasonably likely to be used**; adds that
the existence of additional information enabling identification does not, in itself, make pseudonymised
data personal data **for every person or entity**, and that the Commission, with the EDPB, should support
controllers by stipulating **technical criteria in an implementing act**.
See [GDPR extract, point 1](ST-6406-2026_gdpr-art3-amendments.md#point-1--article-4-definitions) and the
[personal-data provision page](../../docs/provisions/gdpr-art4-personal-data.md).
▸ The operative Article 4(1) addition this recital would have supported is **deleted** (point 1(a)); the
recital nevertheless retains the relative/controller-centric reading and the implementing-act idea.

## Recital 27a (pseudonymisation, singling out, EDPB guidelines)
Retained. The EDPB should support controllers by adopting **guidelines** on the state of the art of
pseudonymisation techniques and on the means reasonably likely to be used to identify a natural person,
including whether data resulting from pseudonymisation may no longer constitute personal data.
▸ Anchors the new Article 70(1)(hca) EDPB-guidelines task (point 14). Note February has **no operative
Article 41a / 29a** (the pseudonymisation article is deleted — see
[GDPR point 10](ST-6406-2026_gdpr-art3-amendments.md#point-10--article-41a-pseudonymisation)); the topic
survives only via this recital and the (hca) guidelines mandate.

## Recital 29 (further processing — archiving / scientific research; Article 89)
Retained. Further processing for archiving in the public interest, scientific or historical research or
statistical purposes is, subject to **Article 89** safeguards, compatible with the initial purposes;
elaborates the conditions for processing to qualify as scientific research (objective characteristics,
methodological/ethical standards, transparency).

## Recital 30 — AI and legitimate interest (RETAINED in February)
**Present.** *Consolidated sense:* trustworthy AI is key to economic growth and socially beneficial
innovation; the development and operation of AI systems and underlying models (e.g. large language models,
generative video models) rely on data, including personal data, across the AI life cycle (training,
testing, validation, and retention in the system/model). Such processing **may therefore be carried out
for a legitimate interest** within the meaning of **Article 6(1)(f)**, where appropriate, without
affecting the controller's obligation to ensure the processing complies with all other Union or national
law and is not explicitly prohibited, and subject to the other conditions and principles of the GDPR.

## Recital 31 — balancing test for AI legitimate interest (RETAINED in February)
**Present.** *Consolidated sense:* when balancing the legitimate interest against the data subject's
interests and fundamental rights, consideration should be given to whether the interest is beneficial for
the data subject and society at large (e.g. improving accessibility), to the **reasonable expectations**
of the data subject, and to **appropriate safeguards** — including enhanced transparency, an
**unconditional right to object**, limiting the use of data for AI development by third parties,
state-of-the-art privacy-preserving techniques for AI training, and technical measures to minimise risks
from **regurgitation, data leakage** and other foreseeable actions.

▸ **Major Feb-vs-May divergence.** Recitals 30 and 31 are **both present in February**, supporting the
standalone operative AI legitimate-interest article (**Article 88c**, also present here — see
[GDPR point 15](ST-6406-2026_gdpr-art3-amendments.md#point-15--articles-88-machine-readable-consent)). By
May, **recitals 30 and 31 are deleted** and Article 88c is deleted; the AI legitimate-interest statement
survives there only as **recital 33a**. See
[May recitals](ST-9547-2026_recitals.md#recital-33a-ai-legitimate-interest) and the
[AI LI provision page](../../docs/provisions/gdpr-art88c-ai-legitimate-interest.md).

## Recital 32 (scientific research as legitimate interest; Article 89(1))
Present. Processing for scientific-research purposes and the application of the GDPR's research
provisions are conditional on **Article 89(1)** safeguards; such research may, where relevant, be
necessary for the purposes of a legitimate interest under Article 6(1)(f) pursued by a controller or
third party, provided the research is not contrary to Union or Member State law and serves the public
interest, and provided the Article 6(1)(f) balancing and the Regulation's other requirements are met.

## Recital 33 (special categories of personal data in AI; Article 9(2) derogation)
Present. Development of AI systems/models may involve special categories of personal data residually
existing in training/testing/validation datasets; a **derogation from the Article 9(1) prohibition**
(operative Article 9(2)(k)) should be allowed where the controller implements appropriate technical and
organisational measures to avoid processing special categories, **removes** any such data identified, or
— where removal requires disproportionate effort — effectively protects it from being used to infer
outputs, disclosed or made available to third parties. The derogation does not apply where processing
special categories is itself the purpose.
See [special-categories provision page](../../docs/provisions/gdpr-art9-special-categories-ai.md).
▸ Uses **"remove"** (the May operative text changes this to **"erase"**) and does not yet carry May's
explicit regular-verification + documentation duty.

## Recital 34 (biometric verification; Article 4(14))
Present. Supports operative Article 9(2)(l): processing of biometric data for **one-to-one verification**
of identity, under the **sole control and possession** of the data subject and subject to Union or Member
State law providing appropriate safeguards (e.g. biometric data stored solely on the data subject's own
device).

## Recital 35 (manifestly unfounded / excessive / abusive access requests; Article 15)
Present. Supports operative Article 12(5): where requests are manifestly unfounded or excessive, or where
an **abusive intention** on the part of the data subject can be demonstrated (in particular for Article 15
access requests submitted for purposes other than data protection, or repeated identical/similar
requests), the controller may charge a reasonable fee or refuse, bearing the **burden of proof**.
See [abusive-requests provision page](../../docs/provisions/gdpr-art12-57-abusive-requests.md).
▸ Unlike May, February carries **no parallel Article 57(4)** amendment for supervisory authorities (see
[GDPR point 11](ST-6406-2026_gdpr-art3-amendments.md#point-11--article-57-supervisory-authority-requests)).

## Recital 36 (information-obligation exemption; Article 13(4))
Present. Supports operative Article 13(4): the duty to inform may be disapplied where the data subject
already has the information and the data are collected in a **direct, limited and clearly circumscribed**
relationship with a controller whose activity is not likely to result in a high risk and does not involve
large amounts of personal data, special categories, or complex processing.

## Recital 37 (research exemption from information duty; Article 13(5))
Present. Supports operative Article 13(5): where further processing for scientific research by the same
controller makes providing the information impossible or disproportionate, the controller need not provide
it, subject to Article 89(1) safeguards and measures such as making the information publicly available.

## Recital 38 — Article 22 (automated individual decision-making) — [DELETED in ST 6406/26]
The Commission's recital justifying the rewrite of **Article 22** (decisions based solely on automated
processing; the "regardless of whether the decision could be taken otherwise than by solely automated
means" / "less intrusive solution" rationale) is **struck out in its entirety**.
▸ Consistent with the operative **Article 22 amendment also being deleted** in February (the article
remains unchanged from the current GDPR — see
[GDPR point 7](ST-6406-2026_gdpr-art3-amendments.md#point-7--article-22-automated-decisions)). The same
recital is also deleted in May.

## Recital 39 (breach notification; higher threshold; single-entry point; Article 33)
Present. Supports operative Article 33: to reduce burden while keeping authorities informed, the
notification threshold is **aligned to that of communication to the data subject (high risk)** under
Article 34; the higher threshold does not affect the controller's documentation duty; and the **Board**
should **establish and make public** a common breach-notification template and the high-risk /
not-high-risk lists. Reviewed every three years.
See [breach-notification provision page](../../docs/provisions/gdpr-art33-breach-notification.md).
▸ The routing through a **single-entry point (Article 23a NIS2)** is referred to here and in Article 33,
but the entry point itself is **not established** by any operative article in February (no cyber Articles
6–9 — see [cyber extract](ST-6406-2026_cyber-art6-9.md)).

## Recital 40 (DPIA lists; Article 35)
Present. Supports operative Article 35: the **Board** establishes and makes public a single EU-level list
of processing operations requiring (and not requiring) a DPIA, plus a common template and methodology,
replacing divergent national lists; existing national lists remain valid until then.

## Recital 44 (terminal equipment / cookies — kept in the GDPR in February)
Present. *Consolidated sense:* the storing of, or access to, personal data in a natural person's
**terminal equipment** and the subsequent processing should be regulated under a **single legal
framework, namely Regulation (EU) 2016/679** — i.e. the new **GDPR Article 88a** — anchoring consent to
the GDPR and setting out the closed list of consent-exempt purposes (transmission; explicitly-requested
service; first-party audience measurement; security of the service/terminal equipment).
See [cookies provision page](../../docs/provisions/gdpr-art88a-88b-cookies.md).
▸ **Feb-vs-May divergence.** February locates the cookie-consent regime **in the GDPR (Article 88a)**;
May moves it **back into the ePrivacy Directive (Article 5(3))** and deletes Article 88a. See
[ePrivacy extract](ST-6406-2026_eprivacy-art5.md).

## Recital 45 (consent fatigue / no-repeat after refusal)
Present. Data subjects faced with repeated consent requests may suffer detrimental effects; after a
refusal the controller should not make a new request for the same purpose for **at least six months**.

## Recital 46 (automated and machine-readable indications of choice; web browsers)
Present. *Consolidated sense:* data subjects should be able to rely on **automated, machine-readable
signals** to give, refuse or withdraw consent or to object; these may be implemented in **web browser**
settings or, e.g., the EU Digital Identity Wallet (Regulation (EU) 910/2014); controllers should respect
such signals; a **media-service-provider exemption** applies; market-driven solutions with appropriate
interfaces should be supported pending standards.
▸ Supports operative **Article 88b** (machine-readable signals), which is **retained into May**
(renumbered, and there extended to operating-system providers).

## Recital 47 (ePrivacy Directive 2002/58/EC; Article 5(3))
Present. The ePrivacy Directive's protections (incl. confidentiality of communications) are preserved;
the existing **Article 5(3) of Directive 2002/58/EC should remain applicable** insofar as the subscriber
or user is **not a natural person** (for natural persons, the new GDPR Article 88a applies).

## Recital 48 (repeal of ePrivacy Article 4)
Present. **Article 4 of Directive 2002/58/EC** (security of processing; breach notification for providers
of electronic communications services) **should be repealed** — the subject matter is covered by NIS2 and
by GDPR Articles 32–34 (5(2)/34) and the broader GDPR rules.
▸ Stated in the recital only; **no operative amending article** carries it out in this text (see
[ePrivacy extract](ST-6406-2026_eprivacy-art5.md)).

---

## Recital 59 (repeal of the Platform-to-Business Regulation (EU) 2019/1150)
Present. The Digital Services Act (EU) 2022/2065 and Digital Markets Act (EU) 2022/1925 provide a
harmonised framework largely covering the matters and protections of the **P2B Regulation**, which
**should be repealed**; for legal certainty and to keep the necessary protection for business users,
**selected provisions** (definitions in Article 2, the restriction/suspension rules in Article 4, the
internal complaint-handling system in Article 11, and Article 15 on enforcement) that are cross-referenced
by, or not covered by, other acts will **temporarily remain in application until 2032**.
See [final provisions](ST-6406-2026_final-art10-11.md#article-10-amendments-repeals-transitory) and the
[repealed-instruments page](../../docs/instruments/repealed.md).

## Recital 61 (legal basis)
Present. The amendments to Regulation (EU) 2016/679 and Regulation (EU) 2018/1725 are based on
**Article 16 TFEU**; the amendments to Directive 2002/58/EC are based on Article 16 TFEU and Article 114
TFEU; all other amendments are based on **Article 114 TFEU**.
