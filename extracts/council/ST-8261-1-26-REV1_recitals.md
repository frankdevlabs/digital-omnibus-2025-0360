# Part 1 — Recitals 1–26 (Data Act / data-acquis consolidation; new in April)

## Recitals 1–3 — simplification framing
*Consolidated sense:* following the Commission's "A simpler and faster Europe" communication (COM(2025) 47)
and pending a forthcoming **Digital Fitness Check**, this Regulation makes a **first set** of amendments to
the digital legislative framework to provide immediate regulatory clarifications, cut administrative
compliance costs (especially for business) and streamline supervisory costs, while maintaining the highest
standards of privacy and data protection.

<a id="recital-4-two-act-consolidation"></a>
## Recital 4 — consolidation into two acts (the structural keystone)
*Consolidated sense:* given the foundational role of data, the data-related amendments seek to **consolidate
the data regulatory framework into only two legal acts — Regulation (EU) 2016/679 (GDPR) and Regulation
(EU) 2023/2854 (Data Act) — down from the current five**, cutting administrative costs and stimulating data
availability while preserving privacy, personal-data protection and fair business practices.
▸ This is the rationale for the Data Act becoming the consolidating home (Omnibus Article 1) and for the
repeal of the DGA, FFNPD and Open Data Directive (Omnibus Article 10) — see
[Data Act instrument page](../../docs/instruments/data-act-2023-2854.md) and
[repealed instruments](../../docs/instruments/repealed.md).

## Recitals 5–8 — Data Governance Act regimes folded in; data intermediation streamlined
*Consolidated sense:* the **DGA (Reg (EU) 2022/868)** established three regimes — (a) re-use of protected
public-sector data, (b) **data intermediation services**, and (c) **data altruism** — whose interplay with
the **Open Data Directive (Dir (EU) 2019/1024)** caused confusion; the two sets of rules should be **merged**
(recital 7). The data-intermediation regime is **streamlined** (recital 8): the provider definition is made
more precise; the **compulsory registration regime becomes voluntary**; the **legal-separation** requirement
from other value-added services is replaced by a **functional separation** with safeguards; and national
registers are replaced by **Union-only public registers** (one for data intermediation service providers,
one for data altruism organisations). Certain DGA data-altruism obligations (national policies, the
'rulebook', the EU consent form) are dropped as unnecessary.

## Recitals 9–14 — Data Act trade-secret protection / third-country access
*Consolidated sense:* the Data Act (Chapter II) requires data holders to make data available, including
trade-secret-protected data, subject to confidentiality safeguards. A **new ground to refuse access** is
introduced where disclosure could expose trade secrets to **third-country regimes with inadequate,
non-equivalent or unenforced protection** (recitals 11, 14), supplementing the existing "serious economic
damage" ground; refusals must be **duly substantiated, notified to the competent authority, treated as
confidential**, and are challengeable by users/third parties.

## Recital 15 — business-to-government data sharing narrowed to "public emergencies"
*Consolidated sense:* Chapter V of the Data Act (B2G data sharing) is **narrowed from "exceptional need" to
"public emergencies"**, reducing burden; the role of official statistics (Reg (EC) No 223/2009) is preserved
for such situations, and **micro/small enterprises required to provide data may claim compensation**.

<a id="recital-16-smart-contracts"></a>
## Recital 16 — Article 36 (smart contracts) deleted
*Consolidated sense:* the smart-contract requirements (Article 36 of the Data Act) — robustness, access
control, consistency with contractual terms, a safe termination/interruption mechanism — lacked harmonised
standards and clear definitions and were potentially **incompatible with decentralised/public blockchain
(immutable-ledger) architectures**; **Article 36 is eliminated** to foster innovation and new business models.

## Recitals 17–18 — custom-built data processing services; SME/SMC switching regimes
*Consolidated sense:* **custom-made data processing services** (not off-the-shelf, contracts concluded
before/on 12 September 2025) are largely **carved out of Chapter VI** (except the duty to reduce/remove
switching and egress charges); providers that are **SMEs/SMCs** get a **lighter regime** for pre-existing
non-IaaS contracts (incl. proportionate early-termination penalties), without undermining the gradual
withdrawal of switching/egress charges (contractual terms contrary to that objective are deemed never to
have existed).

## Recitals 19–20 — Free Flow of Non-Personal Data Regulation repealed; data localisation
*Consolidated sense:* the **FFNPD Regulation (Reg (EU) 2018/1807)** is **repealed**; its core **no-data-
localisation / free-flow principle is retained in the Data Act**, with the Commission continuing to monitor
deviations, and data-localisation requirements justified on **public-security** grounds (Art 52 TFEU) must
be suitable and proportionate (recital 20).

## Recitals 21–22 — Open Data Directive + DGA public-sector re-use consolidated into Chapter VIIc
*Consolidated sense:* both the **Open Data Directive (Dir (EU) 2019/1024)** and Chapter II of the **DGA**
regulate re-use of public-sector information; their overlap created legal uncertainty for public-sector
bodies, so the rules are **aligned and consolidated into a single Chapter (VIIc) of the Data Act**, while
respecting national organisational specificities and leaving third-party intellectual-property rights
unaffected.

## Recitals 23–26 — further Data Act consolidation detail
*Consolidated sense:* additional clarifications on the consolidated public-sector-data-reuse regime, scope
of protected data, competent bodies and transitional matters accompany the Chapter VIIc consolidation. (Curated
at a high level here; the operative text is in the
[Data Act extract](ST-8261-1-26-REV1_dataact-art1.md).)

## Recital 26a — European Data Innovation Board — [NEW in ST 8261/1/26 REV 1]
**Wholly new recital.** *Consolidated sense:* the **European Data Innovation Board's** character as a
**consultative body** for the implementation and enforcement of the Data Act is **maintained** but its
structure is **simplified** to allow more strategic discussion; technical exchanges and dialogue between
national enforcement bodies continue in subgroups, which may also discuss the newly added **Chapters VIIa
and VIIc**.

# Part 2 — Recitals 27+ (GDPR / ePrivacy / cyber strand)

## Recital 27 — concept of personal data — amended (reduced to its opening sentence)
The opening sentence is **kept**: *"This Regulation proposes a series of targeted amendments to Regulation
(EU) 2016/679 for clarification and simplification, whilst preserving the same level of data protection."*
The long Commission passage that followed — reframing personal data toward a **relative / controller-centric**
concept (information "is not to be considered personal data for a given entity" lacking the means to
identify; later transmission to a third party with such means; the Commission/EDPB implementing-act support)
— is **struck out**.
▸ Consistent with the operative **Article 4(1) addition being deleted**
([GDPR point 1](ST-8261-1-26-REV1_gdpr-art3-amendments.md#point-1--article-4-definitions)). See
[personal-data provision page](../../docs/provisions/gdpr-art4-personal-data.md).

<a id="recital-27a-pseudonymisation"></a>
## Recital 27a — identifiability / pseudonymisation / EDPB opinion
*Consolidated sense:* to determine whether a natural person is identifiable, account should be taken of all
the **means reasonably likely to be used**, directly or indirectly; identification should be assessed
**ex ante and in concreto**, considering the controller's actual technical, organisational and legal
capabilities. The **European Data Protection Board** should ensure consistency and support controllers by
adopting an **opinion on pseudonymisation and anonymisation** — assessing the state of the art and the
technical/organisational measures and criteria to apply pseudonymisation effectively, and clarifying when it
may prevent persons other than the controller from identifying the data subject — after a **public
consultation**; controllers remain fully responsible to determine and demonstrate non-re-identification.
▸ Anchors the operative **Article 29a** EDPB-opinion mechanism
([GDPR point 10](ST-8261-1-26-REV1_gdpr-art3-amendments.md#point-10--article-29a-pseudonymisation)). Unlike
February — whose recital 27a anchored an EDPB *guidelines* task with no operative pseudonymisation article —
this version's 27a anchors the new operative **Article 29a**.

## Recital 28 — scientific-research conditions — [DELETED in ST 8261/1/26 REV 1]
The Commission's recital on assessing whether research meets the conditions of scientific research
(methodological/systematic approach; academic/industry/SME settings; Article 179(2) TFEU; quality and
integrity) is **struck out**.

## Recital 29 — further processing (archiving / research / statistics); Article 89 — retained, amended
*Consolidated sense:* further processing for archiving in the public interest, scientific or historical
research or statistical purposes should be considered compatible; it **should not be** necessary to ascertain
on the basis of Article 6(4) of Regulation (EU) 2016/679 whether the further-processing purpose is
compatible; such further processing **should be considered compatible, provided** it complies with the
principles and the **Article 89** safeguards. The qualification as scientific research should rest on
**objective characteristics** of the research activity (purpose, methodological approach, ethical standards,
transparency, reliability, accountability, oversight, verifiability, research integrity) and not solely on
the controller's declaration. Scientific research should be conducted **autonomously and independently** and
concur to public interest and well-being; it may be conducted in academic, industry and other settings
(incl. SMEs), and its outcome may serve public-interest, private or commercial purposes.

## Recitals 30 & 31 — AI and legitimate interest — [DELETED in ST 8261/1/26 REV 1]
- **Recital 30** (trustworthy AI as economically/socially beneficial; processing personal data across the AI
  life cycle **may be carried out for a legitimate interest** within the meaning of Article 6) is **struck
  out**.
- **Recital 31** (the **balancing test** for AI legitimate interest — benefit to data subject/society,
  reasonable expectations, safeguards such as enhanced transparency, an unconditional right to object, and
  measures against regurgitation/data leakage) is **struck out**.

▸ **Major Feb → Apr → May arc — the central finding on this contested provision.** In **February** both
recitals 30 and 31 were **present**, supporting the standalone operative **Article 88c** (also present in
February). In **this April text** the AI legitimate-interest statement is removed **in its entirety**:
operative Article 88c is **deleted**
([GDPR point 15](ST-8261-1-26-REV1_gdpr-art3-amendments.md#point-15--articles-88-machine-readable-consent)),
recitals 30 and 31 are **deleted**, and there is **no recital 33a** here. The interpretive recital that
restores the statement (**recital 33a**) is a **May** addition — it does **not** exist in April. So, at this
April stage, AI development on personal data falls back on **ordinary Article 6(1)(f)** with no bespoke
recital or article. See [May recitals](ST-9547-2026_recitals.md#recital-33a-ai-legitimate-interest) and the
[AI LI provision page](../../docs/provisions/gdpr-art88c-ai-legitimate-interest.md).

## Recital 32 — scientific research as legitimate interest; Article 89(1) — retained, amended
*Consolidated sense:* processing for scientific research and the GDPR's research provisions are conditional
on **Article 89(1)** safeguards, balancing data protection (Art 8 CFREU) with the freedom of science (Art 13
CFREU); such processing **may be necessary for the interests pursued by a controller or by a third party**
within the meaning of **Article 6(1)(f)**, provided the research is not contrary to Union or Member State
law, and **may also follow public interest** within the meaning of **Article 6(1)(e)** or be based on Member
State and Union law.

## Recital 33 — special categories of personal data in AI; Article 9(2) derogation — retained, amended
*Consolidated sense:* developing AI may involve special categories of personal data **incidentally and
residually** present in training/testing/validation datasets or retained in the system/model, where the
controller has not intended to process them and has taken appropriate measures to avoid it. A **derogation
from the Article 9(1) prohibition** (operative Article 9(2)(k)) should be allowed for such **incidental and
residual** processing — but **not** for special categories collected through **prompts during deployment**.
Where, despite the measures, such data are identified, the controller should **delete** them; if deletion is
impossible or requires manifestly disproportionate effort (e.g. re-engineering the model), it should
effectively protect them from further processing, inference into outputs, disclosure or availability to
third parties, and should have processes to monitor and demonstrate the effectiveness of these measures.
▸ Uses **"delete"** (February used "remove"; May's operative text uses "erase"). See
[special-categories provision page](../../docs/provisions/gdpr-art9-special-categories-ai.md).

## Recital 34 — biometric verification vs identification; Article 9(2)(l) — retained, amended
*Consolidated sense:* biometric recognition includes two distinct functions — **identification** (one-to-many
search against a database) and **verification / authentication** (one-to-one confirmation of a claimed
identity). Derogating from the Article 9(1) prohibition for verification should be subject to appropriate
safeguards under Union or Member State law; controllers should, where possible, prioritise methods not
involving biometric data and choose the **less intrusive** equally-effective means; verification should be
used only where **necessary and proportionate**, and only where the biometric data or the means are under
the **sole control** of the data subject (the data subject can decide when/how they are used; the controller
has no technical capacity to access them in decrypted form or process them outside the strictly limited
comparison) — e.g. stored on the data subject's **device**, or by the controller in state-of-the-art
encrypted form with the key held solely by the data subject.

## Recital 35 — manifestly unfounded / excessive / abusive access requests; Articles 12/15 — retained, amended
*Consolidated sense:* Article 15 gives the right of access; Article 12(5) already provides that where a
request is manifestly unfounded or excessive the controller may charge a reasonable fee or refuse (giving
reasons). A request is **also** to be considered excessive where an **abusive intention** on the part of the
data subject **can be demonstrated by the controller** — e.g. submitting numbers of identical or largely
similar requests with the sole intent of causing harm; offering to withdraw a request for some benefit;
requests made with the sole purpose of obtaining compensation for an alleged infringement deliberately
provoked by the data subject; or exercising a right with the intention to adversely affect a judicial
procedure.
▸ Rebuilt around the **"abusive intention … demonstrated by the controller"** standard (the Commission's
"lower burden of proof" passages are struck). See
[abusive-requests provision page](../../docs/provisions/gdpr-art12-57-abusive-requests.md).

## Recital 35a — Article 57 supervisory-authority requests — [NEW in ST 8261/1/26 REV 1]
**Wholly new recital.** *Consolidated sense:* Article 57 covers manifestly unfounded or excessive requests
(including Article 77 complaints) to the supervisory authority; since Articles 12 and 57 use the same wording
and pursue the same objective (an exception to the free-of-charge principle), the notion of **excessivity in
Article 57 should be adapted likewise**.
▸ Anchors the **new operative Article 57(4)** amendment
([GDPR point 11](ST-8261-1-26-REV1_gdpr-art3-amendments.md#point-11--article-57-supervisory-authority-requests)),
which February did not have.

## Recital 36 — information-obligation exemption; Article 13(4) — retained, amended
*Consolidated sense:* the Article 13(4) derogation is extended to situations where processing is **not likely
to result in a high risk** (Article 35) and there are reasonable grounds to believe the data subject has the
points (a)/(c) information, in a **direct, limited and clearly circumscribed** relationship that **does not
involve processing a large amount of personal data** — e.g. a craftsman and their clients, or associations
and sports clubs managing membership. The derogation must not undermine transparency, applies only to
**foreseeable, non-complex** processing (not in employment, nor in relations with public authorities/bodies),
and is without prejudice to Article 15 access; controllers may use a layered approach to information.

## Recital 37 — research exemption from the information duty; Article 13(5) — retained, amended
*Consolidated sense:* where **further processing by the same controller** for scientific research makes
providing the Article 13 information impossible or disproportionate, it should not be necessary to provide
it; the controller should make reasonable efforts to obtain contact details and otherwise inform data
subjects indirectly (e.g. by making the information publicly available, reaching as many as possible).

<a id="recital-38-article-22"></a>
## Recital 38 — Article 22 (automated individual decision-making) — retained, amended
*Consolidated sense:* Article 22 provides that data subjects have the right **not to be subject to a decision
based solely on automated processing**, except where specific conditions are met. When assessing whether such
a decision is **necessary for entering into, or performance of, a contract** (Article 22(2)(a)), it should
**not be required that the decision could be taken only by solely automated means**: the fact that the
decision could also be taken by a human does not prevent the controller from taking it by solely automated
processing; where several equally effective automated solutions exist, the controller should use the **less
intrusive** one.
▸ **Feb → Apr → May divergence — important.** In **February** recital 38 was **deleted** (and the operative
Article 22 amendment deleted, leaving Article 22 unchanged). In **this April text** recital 38 is **retained
and reworked**, supporting the operative rewrite of Article 22
([GDPR point 7](ST-8261-1-26-REV1_gdpr-art3-amendments.md#point-7--article-22-automated-decisions)). In
**May** the operative Article 22 rewrite is kept but **recital 38 is deleted again**. So April is the only
one of the three versions that carries *both* an operative Article 22 rewrite *and* its supporting recital.
See [automated-decisions provision page](../../docs/provisions/gdpr-art22-automated-decisions.md).

## Recital 39 — breach-notification threshold; EDPB template/lists; Article 33 — retained, amended
*Consolidated sense:* the threshold for notifying a breach to the supervisory authority should be **aligned
to the high-risk threshold** for communication to the data subject (Article 34); the higher threshold does
not affect the duty to **document** the breach (Article 33(5)) or to demonstrate compliance (Article 5(2));
the **Board** should **establish and make public** a common template and the **high-risk / not-high-risk
lists**, reviewed at least every three years, and the **Commission may adopt** the Board-established template
(and its updates) **by implementing act**.
▸ This recital concerns the **threshold and the EDPB template/lists** — not the entry-point routing. See
[breach-notification provision page](../../docs/provisions/gdpr-art33-breach-notification.md).

## Recital 40 — DPIA lists; Article 35 — retained, amended
*Consolidated sense:* a **single EU-level list** of processing operations requiring a DPIA (and, made
mandatory, of those not requiring one) should replace national lists; these lists, plus a common template
and methodology, should be **established and made public by the Board** (taking account of nature, scope,
context, purposes and risk), reviewed at least every three years, and the **Commission may adopt** the
Board-established template (and updates) **by implementing act**.

## Recitals 41–43 — Regulation (EU) 2018/1725 and Directive (EU) 2016/680 alignment
- **(41)** retained, amended — Regulation (EU) 2018/1725 (EU institutions) should be brought into alignment
  with the amendments to the GDPR (the Commission's express coupling with **Directive (EU) 2016/680** is
  struck).
- **(42)** retained — Regulation 2018/1725 is amended in parallel and is to be interpreted homogeneously
  with the GDPR.
- **(43)** — [DELETED in ST 8261/1/26 REV 1] — the Commission's recital on follow-up adaptations of Directive
  (EU) 2016/680 and other acts is **struck out**.

<a id="recital-43a-eprivacy-framework"></a>
## Recital 43a — ePrivacy Directive framework — [NEW in ST 8261/1/26 REV 1]
**Wholly new recital.** *Consolidated sense:* Directive 2002/58/EC ('ePrivacy Directive'), last revised in
2009, provides a framework for the protection of privacy, including the **confidentiality of communications**;
it specifies the GDPR in relation to processing personal data in electronic communications, and protects the
privacy and integrity of the user's/subscriber's terminal equipment.
▸ This is the relocated opening of the old recital 47 (see below), reframed to support keeping the cookie
regime in the **ePrivacy Directive** ([ePrivacy extract](ST-8261-1-26-REV1_eprivacy-art5.md)).

## Recital 44 — terminal equipment / cookies — retained, amended (moved to the ePrivacy Directive)
*Consolidated sense:* the storing of, or access to, personal data in a natural person's **terminal
equipment** and its subsequent processing should be regulated under a **single legal framework** where the
subscriber/user is a natural person; the **amendments to this Directive** (ePrivacy) should continue to offer
the highest protection while simplifying how data subjects exercise rights and express choices online. A
**limitative list** of consent-exempt purposes is defined: **anonymous aggregated audience measurement**
(instantly anonymised, not combined or shared); **security** (only proportionate, discretely packaged
updates that do not change functionality, with prior information and an opt-out); and **contextual
advertising measurement** (based solely on the immediate content, no profiling, no retention or link to past
/ future activity). For **further processing** beyond the list, Article 6 (and, where relevant, Article 9)
apply, including reliance on **legitimate interest** under Article 6(1)(f) subject to safeguards (whether the
data subject is a child; reasonable expectations; scale/sensitivity; data minimisation; no continuous
monitoring of private life), failing which **consent** is needed.
▸ **Feb → Apr → May divergence.** February located the cookie-consent regime **in the GDPR (Article 88a)**;
this April text **moves it into the ePrivacy Directive (Article 5(3))** and reframes recital 44 to speak of
"amendments to this Directive". See [cookies provision page](../../docs/provisions/gdpr-art88a-88b-cookies.md).

## Recital 45 — consent fatigue / no-repeat after refusal — retained, amended
*Consolidated sense:* data subjects who refused consent are often re-prompted on each visit and may consent
just to stop the requests; the controller should be obliged to **respect a refusal for at least a certain
period**. This obligation applies to **any** controller that accesses or stores personal data in the terminal
equipment, **including third-party cookie providers**.

## Recital 46 — automated and machine-readable indications of choice — retained
*Consolidated sense:* data subjects should be able to rely on **automated, machine-readable** indications to
consent, refuse or object; these may be implemented in **web browser** settings or the **EU Digital Identity
Wallet** (Regulation (EU) 910/2014 — the source writes "914/2014"), or other adequate means; controllers
should respect such signals once standards exist; **media service providers** should not be obliged to
respect them (independent journalism).
▸ Supports the retained **machine-readable-signals** article
([GDPR point 15](ST-8261-1-26-REV1_gdpr-art3-amendments.md#point-15--articles-88-machine-readable-consent)).

## Recital 47 — old ePrivacy framing — [DELETED in ST 8261/1/26 REV 1]
The Commission's recital describing the ePrivacy Directive and stating that **Article 5(3) should remain
applicable insofar as the subscriber/user is not a natural person** is **struck out** (its opening is
relocated to the new recital 43a; the natural-person/non-natural-person split is dropped because the regime
now lives in the amended ePrivacy Article 5(3) itself).

## Recital 48 — repeal of ePrivacy Article 4 — retained, amended
*Consolidated sense:* **Article 4 of Directive 2002/58/EC** (security of services; breach notification by
providers of electronic communications services) **should be repealed** — NIS2 now sets the
cybersecurity/incident-reporting requirements and GDPR Articles 32–34 cover security of processing and breach
notification, avoiding overlap; the GDPR rules apply to providers of electronic communications services and
networks.
▸ Carried out by an **operative amending article** in this version
([ePrivacy point 1](ST-8261-1-26-REV1_eprivacy-art5.md#point-1--article-4-deleted)) — February stated the
repeal only in a recital, with no operative article.

## Recital 49 — national entry points for incident reporting — retained, amended
*Consolidated sense:* several Union acts require notifying the same event to different authorities; the
**establishment of points at national level** for incident reporting should let entities fulfil reporting
obligations under NIS2, the GDPR, DORA, eIDAS and CER by submitting to a **single interface at national
level**, with the ability to retrieve previously submitted information.
▸ February referred to a single **"single-entry point"**; this version reframes the recital around **national
entry points** (and a separate ENISA information point — recital 49a).

## Recital 49a — ENISA single information point — [NEW in ST 8261/1/26 REV 1]
**Wholly new recital.** *Consolidated sense:* to facilitate compliance (including identifying applicable
obligations), **ENISA should develop and maintain a single information point** for incident reporting;
structured communication channels should keep it swiftly updated from Member-State information.
▸ Anchors operative **NIS2 Article 23a** ([cyber extract](ST-8261-1-26-REV1_cyber-art6-9.md)).

## Recitals 50 & 51 — security of national entry points; harmonisation — retained, amended
- **(50)** — ENISA should **develop guidelines** addressing appropriate technical/operational/organisational
  measures **for Member States** to develop and maintain their **national entry points** (the Commission's
  ENISA-run single-entry-point security regime is recast).
- **(51)** — supports the **further harmonisation** of incident notification (exchange and interoperability
  of information); ENISA, with the NIS Cooperation Group, should **develop guidelines to foster
  harmonisation** (replacing the Commission's piloting/assessment/notice mechanism).

## Recitals 52, 53, 54 — single-entry-point machinery — [DELETED in ST 8261/1/26 REV 1]
The Commission's recitals on continuity/interoperability with national technical solutions (52), on the
single-entry point enabling the required information/format with ENISA consultation (53), and on aligning
the **DORA** financial-sector reporting framework with the single-entry point (54) are **struck out**
(superseded by the national-entry-point / guidelines model).

## Recitals 55–57 — national entry point: information sharing, channel switch, fallback — retained, amended
- **(55)** — the **national entry point** should be designed to accommodate the subsequent sharing of
  incident-specific information between competent authorities, where a Member State opts to use that feature.
- **(56)** — NIS2, the GDPR, DORA, eIDAS and CER should be amended so reporting is carried out **via the
  national entry point**, which should start being used **within 18 months** from entry into force; if the
  Commission triggers the notice delaying the date of application to **24 months**, the existing
  corresponding provisions continue to apply meanwhile.
- **(57)** — in the exceptional event of a **technical impossibility**, entities should fulfil reporting
  obligations through **alternative means**, which addressees should make publicly available.

## Recital 58 — EDPS / EDPB consultation — retained, amended
*Consolidated sense:* the **European Data Protection Supervisor and the European Data Protection Board** were
consulted in accordance with Article 42 of Regulation (EU) 2018/1725 and delivered their **joint opinion on
10 February 2026**.

<a id="recital-59-p2b"></a>
## Recital 59 — Platform-to-Business Regulation (EU) 2019/1150 — retained, amended
*Consolidated sense:* since the objectives and material provisions of the **P2B Regulation** are largely
covered by the DSA (Regulation (EU) 2022/2065) and DMA (Regulation (EU) 2022/1925), **several provisions of
Regulation (EU) 2019/1150 should be deleted**. For legal certainty and to keep the necessary protection for
**business users**, the recital states that the **terms-and-conditions (Article 3), ranking (Article 5),
differentiated-treatment (Article 7) and enforcement (Article 15) provisions are maintained**, and that the
**Article 4** (restrictions/suspensions) and **Article 11** (internal complaint-handling) provisions — cross-
referenced by, or not covered by, other acts — will **temporarily remain in application until 31 December
2032**.
▸ **Drafting not reconciled with the operative text.** The recital narrates a "remain in application until
31 December 2032" formulation for Articles 4 and 11, whereas operative **Article 10(1)(b)** sets their
**deletion as of 1 January 2033**

## Recital 60 — entry into force — retained
*Consolidated sense:* given the technical nature of the amendments and the urgency of a simplified framework,
this Regulation should **enter into force immediately** after publication, with transitional periods as
appropriate.

## Recital 61 — legal basis — [NEW in ST 8261/1/26 REV 1]
**Wholly new recital.** The amendments to Regulation (EU) 2016/679 and Regulation (EU) 2018/1725 are based on
**Article 16 TFEU**; the amendments to Directive 2002/58/EC are based on **Article 16 TFEU and Article 114
TFEU**; all other amendments are based on **Article 114 TFEU**.
