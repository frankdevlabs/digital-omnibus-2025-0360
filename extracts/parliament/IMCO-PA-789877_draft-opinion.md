## Short justification

While supporting the simplification effort that the Commission proposal aims for, the rapporteur has great reservations when it comes to deregulating or undermining European digital rules. The rapporteur for example welcomes simplifying and bringing the Data Governance Act and the Open Data Directive under one overarching European Data Act.

It is less clear how the deregulatory elements of the proposal like for example the repeal of the Platform to Business Regulation or the changes made to the GDPR and the e-privacy Directive will benefit consumers or the functioning of the internal market. For this reason, the draft IMCO opinion on the Digital Omnibus contains several amendments to bring more balance between simplification and consumer protection. However, these are limited to the competences of IMCO. Parts not amended should not be understood as being supported by the rapporteur.

On the Data Act the draft Opinion aims to clarify the definition of data access and strengthen the definitions of data intermediation services. Also, the rapporteur adds the concept of a data expert representative as a specific kind of regulated data intermediation service that can negotiate under a fiduciary duty on behalf of consumers to attain better terms for their data use. The draft opinion also strengthens the safeguards on what kind of organisations are allowed to register as a data intermediation service. An annual transparency report is required for data altruism organisations to bring accountability, competent authorities can monitor compliance on request of individuals or legal persons, and the draft opinion includes a complaint handling mechanism.

To address the issue of trade secrets being misused as an argument to refuse data access and hamper innovation possibilities for SMEs, the rapporteur includes more safeguards and reincludes some provisions that were deleted in the Commission proposal. Regarding Business to Government data sharing, the draft opinion proposes some changes to address concerns by regulators and some stakeholders.

Concerning the cloud switching rules, the rapporteur seeks to restore the current text under the Data Act to foster more competition, avoid lock-in effects that could favour incumbents and allow companies to find ways to circumvent the switching requirements by claiming to have custom-made services. On smart contracts the draft opinion does not take on board the proposed deletion.

The enforcement of the data acquis is essential and therefore the rapporteur proposes amendments to strengthen the supervision and coordination by competent authorities to ensure more harmonisation and consistency across regulations within the EU single market to benefit companies and consumers alike.

Finally, on the Platform to Business Regulation (P2B) the rapporteur considers there would be gaps by repealing the Regulation, even in two stages. The P2B adds value to SMEs on top of what is in the Digital Services Act and the Digital Markets Act, which have a different scope. P2Br should not be repealed without a proper impact assessment on the effects on SMEs and other European businesses.

In order to determine whether a natural person is identifiable, account** should be **taken of all the means reasonably likely to be used to identify the natural person directly or indirectly. Taking into account** the case-law of the Court of Justice of the European Union **concerning the definition of personal data, it is necessary to provide further clarity on when a natural person should be considered to be identifiable. The existence of additional information enabling the data subject to be identified does not, in itself, mean that pseudonymised data must be regarded as constituting, in all cases and for every person or entity, personal data for the purposes of the application of Regulation (EU) 2016/679. In particular, it should be clarified that information is not to be considered personal data for a given entity where that entity does not have means reasonably likely to be used to identify the natural person to whom the information relates. A potential subsequent transmission of that information to third parties who have means reasonably allowing them to identify the natural person to whom the information relates, such as cross-checking with other data at their disposal, renders that information personal data only for those third parties who have such means at their disposal. An entity for which the information is not personal data, in principle, does not fall** within **the scope of application of Regulation (EU) 2016/679. In this respect the Court of Justice of the European Union has held that a means of identifying the data subject is not reasonably likely to be used where the risk of identification appears in reality to be insignificant, in that the identification of that data subject is prohibited by law or impossible in practice, for example because it would involve a disproportionate effort in terms of time, cost and labour.
