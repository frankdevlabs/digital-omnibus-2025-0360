## Short justification

The rapporteur frames data governance, cloud infrastructure, interoperability and access to data as strategic issues for Europe's technological resilience and economic sovereignty. He accepts the goal of improving coherence and reducing unnecessary burdens on SMEs, but warns that, although the Digital Omnibus is presented as a purely technical and surgical intervention, it in fact affects core definitions, rebalances rights and obligations, and alters the overall balance of the framework without proper political assessment. Simplification, he argues, should not result in legal fragmentation or weaker safeguards in areas essential to trust in the European data economy. The opinion supports a balanced approach — reducing unnecessary complexity while preserving the key principles and governance structures underpinning the Data Act and the Data Governance Act, with the JURI amendments focusing on legal certainty, governance, supervision, contractual fairness, interoperability, and the trustworthiness and neutrality of data-sharing ecosystems.

The justification is organised around themes:

- **Ensuring trustworthy and neutral data-sharing ecosystems** — preserving the neutrality and trustworthiness of data intermediation services by strengthening safeguards on transparency, security, accountability and secondary uses of data, and reinforcing registration and supervision (including harmonised procedures and stronger oversight) to maintain trust and effective supervision within the Union data-sharing ecosystem.
- **Preserving the Union's open data principles** — open government data remains a key driver for research, innovation, education, journalism and public-interest projects. The amendments clarify that the framework does not itself create new disclosure obligations for public sector bodies and remains without prejudice to existing Union and national access and confidentiality regimes; they also preserve non-discriminatory re-use, interoperability and the use of standard and open licences. While it is legitimate to address imbalances linked to the economic power of very large market actors, this should not lead to fragmented licensing practices that undermine cross-border, cross-sector re-use of public sector information.
- **Ensuring balanced and proportionate public-interest access to data (B2G)** — maintaining an appropriate balance between public-interest access and protection of data holders' legitimate interests. Public authorities should retain the possibility to access certain data for clearly defined public-interest purposes, subject to necessity, proportionality and safeguards. The amendments preserve a framework for public-interest access outside emergencies, while clarifying and narrowing certain emergency mechanisms; they remove the broad and ambiguous concept of "mitigation" so that access stays closely linked to concrete emergency response and recovery activities.
- **Trade secrets** — additional safeguards including clearer confidentiality obligations, proportionality requirements and guidance on protecting commercially sensitive information in cross-border contexts. Refusal of access should remain a measure of last resort, with identified risks addressed first through proportionate technical, organisational and contractual safeguards rather than broad precautionary refusals that undermine Data Act access rights.
- **Preserving fairness and transparency in platform-to-business relations** — the proposed repeal of the P2B Regulation raises concerns. The DSA and DMA pursue different objectives and do not fully replace the broader fairness and transparency safeguards available to SMEs and business users, notably on ranking transparency, complaint-handling and safeguards against unfair suspension practices.
- **Cloud switching** — preserving the effectiveness of cloud switching and interoperability obligations to reduce vendor lock-in and maintain fair competition. While the need to reduce disproportionate burdens for SMEs and providers of tailored services is recognised, broad exemptions risk weakening portability rights and creating fragmented switching regimes.
- **Smart contracts** — the proposed deletion of the smart-contract provisions risks removing important minimum safeguards for automated data-sharing agreements. While legal-uncertainty and compliance concerns should be addressed, minimum requirements on security, interoperability, controllability and auditability remain important for trust, legal certainty and cross-platform compatibility.
- **Strengthening legal certainty and the coordinating role of the EDIB** — maintaining legal certainty and coherent implementation across Member States by strengthening coordination mechanisms, clarifying responsibilities between competent authorities, and reinforcing the role of the European Data Innovation Board in supporting guidance, cooperation and consistent implementation, including in cross-border situations.
